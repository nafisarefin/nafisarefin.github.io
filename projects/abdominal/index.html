Hemodynamics and Emboli Transport in Abdominal Arteries During Cardiopulmonary Bypass

This research investigates how emboli, capable of causing organ injury, travel through the descending aorta during cardiopulmonary bypass (CPB), a life-saving surgical procedure. While prior studies focused on the brain and heart, this work explores how emboli reach and affect abdominal organs like the kidneys and liver. By simulating a full-length patient-specific aortic anatomy, this study reveals how CPB conditions such as blood viscosity and flow rate influence embolic risk to these critical organs, with the goal of informing safer surgical strategies.

A patient-specific computational model of the thoracic and abdominal aorta was developed. Embolic transport was simulated using CFD and Lagrangian Particle Tracking (LPT) in OpenFOAM, validated through prior in vitro experiments. Simulations were conducted across six CPB scenarios combining variable pump flow rates (3–5 LPM), blood viscosities (1.5–3.5 cP), and embolus sizes (0.5–2.5 mm).

This project demonstrates proficiency in CAD modeling, OpenFOAM-based CFD simulation, mesh convergence analysis, LPT configuration, and hemodynamic post-processing. Results showed that emboli transport increased significantly under lower viscosity and higher flow rate, up to 27% for renal and 10.7% for hepatic arteries. The findings align with clinical reports of CPB-related acute kidney and liver injuries, and offer a new mechanistic foundation for predicting and mitigating abdominal organ damage during cardiac surgery.

Preprint:
